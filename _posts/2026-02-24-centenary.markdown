**Published in “A New Ulster “ Magazine \- Issue 63 – 2017**

Made it\! – pay for ticket, grab it, stick it<br />
into checking machine, pull it out, push<br />
through into Station, run onto platform –<br />
right, train just curving down the line<br />
from Balbriggan towards Skerries- stand<br />
catching my breath and watching the<br />
new green leaves of Spring on the trees<br />
up the road towards Milverton, white<br />
clouds piling in from the sea ....and<br />
“Please Stand Behind Yellow line”, more<br />
gold than yellow... as the train door<br />
hisses open , and I step on board, look<br />
for seat at window, all taken, hate sitting<br />
on the outside but can't be helped,<br />
woman staring out the window, flicking<br />
a glance as I search for notebook,<br />
fountain pen (love fountain-pens) \-<br />
would really prefer to be on the inside<br />
myself but anyway – train moving<br />
slowly as Windmill, Watermill, Mill<br />
Pond, Martello Tower on Shenick Island<br />
slide past across the aisle \- scribble a bit<br />
\- people talking on mobiles, talking to<br />
one another, young man with woolly hat,<br />
“She threw me out, but I have to see the<br />
baby, the Judge said ”, and we're<br />
beginning to pickupspeed – other<br />
young man, “Me, I spent St. Patrick's<br />
Day in the Garda Station – me sister<br />
came and bailed me out” \- beautifullycut<br />
black hair, designer stubble, and I<br />
watch as the smooth green Golf Course<br />
flies past, and through the opposite<br />
window, the Lighthouse faint in mist out<br />
at sea , and a girl in the seat across from<br />
me taps at her laptop, red hair, tan boots,<br />
long coat \- black, \- mine dark blue,<br />
scarf, white woolly hat that I stuff into<br />
my bag, warm high boots, writing fast,<br />
trying to get it all down as I will try for<br />
the next forty-or-so minute journey to<br />
Dublin , gold fountain-pen nib moving<br />
fast, moving towards Clontarf (Hello\!) ,<br />
Connolly, Pearse \- but first rushing<br />
towards Rush (hah\!) and Lusk... and I'm<br />
watching out for the castle on the hill,<br />
something...something \- Bal \-<br />
Baldungan \- supposed to have been built<br />
by the Knights Templar, the warrior<br />
monks \- must look them up \- and<br />
the train begins to slow down , no<br />
rushing now (snigger) and then<br />
stops , the car \-park full, red, white,<br />
blue ...cars left earlier by commuters<br />
working in the City...and a hiss and<br />
some boys and girls get on, going to<br />
College, maybe...laughing, shiny hair,<br />
laptops out of satchels, fingers flying<br />
already, and the door closes and we<br />
are off again....slowly gathering<br />
speed.... all the stations, all the stops and<br />
all the people \- Portrane coming into<br />
view, Lambay Island off the coast \- learnt<br />
about that at school \- the Vikings<br />
landing on Lambay Island, the Vikings<br />
coming up the Shannon to Limerick too.<br />
Lom na n-each, The Bare Place of the<br />
Horses, \- Limerick, a sore place in my<br />
heart, the City of Pogroms and Sodalities,<br />
of long summer days by the Shannon, of<br />
primroses and violets in Spring, of pussywillow<br />
and corncrakes, the City of the<br />
Broken Treaty – broken \- leave that ,<br />
don't think about all that ...the<br />
train fillingup, the early passengers going<br />
to jobs, more and more again finding<br />
work in Dublin as the recession ends, but<br />
not down here, says my brother-in-law,<br />
no recovery down here as he tries to keep<br />
a family firm going, importing oak and<br />
mahogony from all over the world , trees<br />
that once covered Ireland , “cad a<br />
dhéanfhaimíd feasta gan adhmaid “–<br />
seized by England for England's<br />
Elizabethan ships, that garnered (great<br />
word that\!) an Empire for that small<br />
country ..... and I hope I'll be able to read<br />
my awful scrawl back, the result of all<br />
those English compositions years ago at<br />
school \- and outside an old grey stone<br />
house, some glass houses pewter under a<br />
leaden sky, golden furse in the ditches \-<br />
or gorse- or whin \- gorse we called it<br />
down our way \- always loved that nutty<br />
almondy scent \- green spiky leaves,<br />
white daisies in the fields, clusters of<br />
tiny golden flowers ...all of us...all of us<br />
walking out into the country, kettle, pan,<br />
collecting wood, making tea, boiled<br />
potatoes for frying, sausages, jelly and<br />
custard in jam-jars \- all of us.......and<br />
there's a notice up on the carriage wall \-<br />
“Réabhlóid 1916, Rebellion 19l6 \- Come<br />
to Dublin, Experience the Pageants, the<br />
Exhibitions , the Re-enactments “ \- an<br />
invitation to the Nation to celebrate... no,<br />
I prefer commemorate \- Easter Monday<br />
1916 when a small group of men and<br />
women \- extraordinary men and women \-<br />
poets, writers, actors, dreamers, rose up<br />
against England, while the Great War<br />
raged, The War to End All Wars,<br />
“England's difficulty, Ireland's<br />
opportunity”, a ferment of ideas that<br />
brought about a change, the plays and the<br />
poems – and “Step into History” \- that<br />
billboard on the wall again...all that...and<br />
again the train slowing down at<br />
Donabate but not so many passengers<br />
now...all that energy, all over<br />
Ireland, clubs and organizations ,<br />
feminist groups, political groups \-<br />
G.A.A. , Cumann na mBan, National<br />
Volunteers, National Women's Franchise<br />
League, Irish Volunteers \- and the Abbey<br />
Theatre a melting pot (cliche that \- but I<br />
like it, nice rhythm, ”mel \- ting – pot”)<br />
anyway, a list as long as your arm of<br />
actors of both sexes, playwrights, screen<br />
painters, wardrobe mistresses, all<br />
working towards political freedom,<br />
religious freedom, personal freedom, and<br />
\- “Did a play of mine send those men out<br />
to die?“ \- ah, for feck's sake, Mr. Yeats,<br />
it wasn't just your play and anyway,<br />
Lady Gregory wrote most of it\! – and<br />
the train pulls away from Donabate,<br />
matted couch-grass on the sides of the<br />
high banks and the moving red digital<br />
line over the door into the next carriage,<br />
\- An Céad Stáisiún – the next Station \-<br />
“Jesus Falls the Second Time” – moving<br />
down through the Church, praying at<br />
each Station – each station bringing me<br />
nearer to the City, \- the Eternal City? \-<br />
(Getting carried away with yourself now,<br />
Marie\!) ) \- and then the Malahide<br />
embankment, sheets of silver-grey water<br />
on each side, marshy land, water holes<br />
and grey old reeds, no new life yet,<br />
boatyards, apartments, \- thinking of the<br />
chat and talk in Dublin , about books, the<br />
work, the hopes – and again a hiss and a<br />
stop , people getting on and off, and a<br />
hiss again \- sky still grey, the train<br />
running between high banks of old<br />
bracken now , trees skeletal here , the<br />
low sun of Spring not yet able to do the<br />
work of regeneration, and we're slowing<br />
towards Portmarnock, and yellow lines<br />
again ... Please Stand Behind... man on<br />
platform, grey hair, bomber jacket,<br />
grumpy – old , old station hut, grey<br />
stone, fluted wooden ornamentation on<br />
peak of roof beside car park “Stad<br />
Carranna – Car Stop? \- Carranna,? \-<br />
Carrana? ... what happened to<br />
“Gluaisteáin? \- many of us still not able<br />
to speak our own language after nearly<br />
thirteen years of intensive learning at<br />
school – but book learning – no<br />
conversation and no fun at all\! \- unless<br />
you were lucky and joined some kind of<br />
Irish-speaking group and lived in the<br />
language \- and outside now new houses,<br />
hipped roofs, cream – row after<br />
row.....and apartments – but half-built \-<br />
the ghosts of the Celtic Tiger boom times<br />
– young people living in half-finished<br />
estates, children playing against high<br />
metal fences, the dream of your own<br />
house, your own place \- but stranded<br />
now, no chance of selling up...who would<br />
want to buy here? ..and more and more<br />
houses , older, inhabited, the greypainted<br />
stairs over the railway lines at<br />
Howth Junction, girl standing reading on<br />
the platform as we go by, boots, a miniskirt<br />
(never go out of fashion\!) and the<br />
train rushing towards the City, station<br />
after station, no stopping now, churches,<br />
factories, the Dublin Mountains in the<br />
distance, the Liffey on the<br />
left......beginning to get frazzled , trying<br />
to catch it all , the ideas, images ,<br />
memories passing through my mind –<br />
over a bridge, a small river, Poolbeg twin<br />
chimney stacks coming nearer, houses to<br />
the left, to the right, big, small, streets<br />
winding through, and the woman on my<br />
left still glumly looking out the windowwhen<br />
I could perhaps have passed the<br />
time of day with her, talked about this<br />
and that, even heard her story – rattling<br />
across that old bridge , always nervously,<br />
\- but I am too busy getting it down,<br />
making it happen on the page, and I and<br />
I ...I'm tired now and want to finish, but I<br />
won't let myself, I'll go on......passengers<br />
chatting, boy pulling out ear-phones,<br />
woman settling her hat, putting on<br />
gloves, girls flicking hair, laughing,<br />
excited...very near Dublin now, only<br />
Connolly, Tara and then finally....<br />
..Pearse, the station named after one of<br />
the Signatories of the Proclamation , the<br />
visionary \- the teacher \- author of the<br />
book, “The Murder Machine”, not about<br />
the Blood Sacrifice, although he wrote<br />
about that too, put words into his mother's<br />
mouth, “I do not grudge them, Lord, I do<br />
not grudge my two strong sons” ... but I<br />
would have grudged mine, and my two<br />
precious daughters too,<br />
screeched against my great loss...but she<br />
was proud of her sacrifice, they say, God<br />
help her....a different time.... slowing<br />
down now.... slowing down and<br />
stopping at Connolly Station , James<br />
Connolly the workers' champion ,<br />
wounded at the Post Office, carried out,<br />
seated in a chair.... shot dead by the<br />
British........and as the train slowly glides<br />
away towards Butt Bridge, I am excited<br />
again, as always, from when I was at<br />
school and loved history and loved the<br />
idea of Dublin, all the buildings, all the<br />
lives lived, all the....”the passing parade”<br />
\- me, fourteen \- standing outside the<br />
Gaiety Theatre , brought by my aunt all<br />
the way from Limerick to see an<br />
opera....watching it all....and the train is<br />
edging into the Centre of the City, the<br />
high blocks of offices beginning their<br />
stately waltz, showing first one<br />
facade...and then another.... as the<br />
railway line curves towards the iron<br />
bridge , the glass pagoda of Liberty Hall<br />
silver-pewter under the grey sky.... green<br />
white and gold flag flying over the<br />
Custom's House ....and there's the Liffey<br />
below, streets either side crowded with<br />
cars, lorries, buses, traffic lights green ,<br />
the white paper of a new page catching<br />
the gathering words, the gold nib<br />
flashing, as the train enters Tara<br />
Street Station, (the harp that once<br />
....hah\!) ...and again the carriage door<br />
hissing open, people jostling, rushing ...<br />
door closing, and the high buildings<br />
graceful, a slow waltz again, the street<br />
below swaying between shops and pubs<br />
and offices as the train glides into<br />
Pearse Street Station , quiet now at this<br />
mid-morning hour, passengers gathering<br />
bags, closing down laptops, pigeons<br />
fluttering towards the Victorian glass and<br />
iron roof as the train slows down<br />
and stops ...and I stop. End of the line.<br />
